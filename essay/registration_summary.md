**Digital Land: Who Captures the Value When the Cloud Touches the Ground?**

Georgia has become the country's fastest-growing new data-center market: state
auditors count 63 active facilities, 35 under construction, and 249 more
announced, while the Public Service Commission has certified 9,885 MW of new
generation — roughly 80 percent of it for data centers. My entry asks a Georgist
question of this boom: when a data-center announcement lands in a Georgia
community, who captures the value it creates — and who bears the risk if that
value never materializes? I build an original, hand-verified dataset of dated
Georgia data-center announcements (2019–2026) and estimate how announcements
capitalize into nearby residential property values, using Zillow's ZIP-level
home value index and the Callaway–Sant'Anna staggered difference-in-differences
estimator. Henry George's savannah parable frames the design: an announcement is
community-created land value compressed into a single dated event, accruing to
whoever holds title. I pair these estimates with documented institutional facts
— a sales-tax exemption costing roughly $2.5 billion a year, ratepayer-backstopped
generation for load that may never arrive — and an illustrative stranded-cost
simulation. Land value capture, not equipment subsidies, is the remedy George
would recognize.
